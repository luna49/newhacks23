Thevenin Equivalent Circuits





What is a Thevenin Equivalent Circuit, and why do we need to know it?

The general idea is that any linear circuit can be reduced to a single voltage source and resistor in parallel, as seen from some set of ports. They are helpful for determining the overall behavior of a certain portion of a larger circuit.

How do we solve for the Thevenin Equivalent?

Depending on the type of circuit, we start by solving for either V_oc or R_th.

When you first look at a Thevenin problem, classify the circuit as one of the following:
  • Case 1: The circuit contains only independent sources.
  • Case 2: The circuit contains only dependent sources.
  • Case 3: The circuit contains both independent and dependent sources.
    •

    Case 1: All-Independent Sources

    This is the simplest case. For these circuits, you can find your V_th by opening the terminals you are looking at, and finding the open-circuit voltage between them. Use analysis techniques like nodal or mesh to find this.

    As for your R_th, you can find it by:
    1. Deactivating your sources (opening current sources, shorting voltage sources)
    2. Finding the equivalent resistance (now that there are only resistors in your circuit), with respect to the same terminals

    Case 2: All-Dependent Sources

    In this case, you can solve for both V_th and R_th by placing a test source (current or voltage) with a value of 1V or 1A across the terminals we are looking at.

    Note: the polarity of test voltage sources must match V_oc, and test current sources must flow from the negative terminal to the positive terminal (orientation matters, so be careful).

    Then, find both the voltage and current across that test source, and use $\frac{V}{I}$ to find R_th.

    We can do this because the circuit contained only dependent sources. It would not have produced any response unless there were other responses caused by an independent source.
